# Au Train Strandplain Evolution

## Summary — Evolution Through Time (10 ka → Present)

Over the last 10,000 years, the Au Train–Copper Harbor region along Lake Superior has experienced dramatic changes in both lake level and land elevation. Immediately after the last glaciers retreated (~10 ka), the crust was still heavily depressed under the former ice load, and the lake surface stood far below today’s level. As ice melted and outlets shifted, the water first rose rapidly, then fell again as new drainage paths opened. The land, meanwhile, rebounded steadily upward as the weight of ice was removed.

The result is a landscape of ancient beach ridges and swales — a *strandplain* — that records the complex history of shoreline migration. Around 8–4.5 ka (the **Nipissing phase**), the lake reached its local highstand about 5 m above today’s surface, while the land beneath Copper Harbor was still ~2 m lower than today. The net effect placed the shoreline roughly 7 m higher than the modern lake, flooding lowlands and depositing beach ridges that now stand a few meters above the current shore. As lake levels later fell and land rebound continued, the modern shoreline emerged and stabilized into the form we see today.

---

## Water-Level History

The water level of ancestral Lake Superior rose and fell repeatedly during the Holocene as ice-dammed outlets opened and closed. Early in the deglaciation (10–9 ka), meltwater pooled in the basin while southern outlets stood high, creating relatively low lake levels. When those outlets were blocked by ice or sediment, water levels rose rapidly, sometimes several centimeters per year.

Around 8–4.5 ka, during the **Nipissing transgression**, the lake reached its highest local level, about 5 m above today. Following that highstand, isostatic rebound around the northern outlets (St. Marys River and Ottawa-Mattawa system) and regional drainage changes caused a steady fall in lake level, eventually settling near modern elevation.

---

## Land-Level (Isostatic) Rebound

As the Laurentide Ice Sheet melted, the Earth's crust beneath the Upper Peninsula rose elastically and viscously — a process called **glacial isostatic adjustment** (GIA). At Au Train and Copper Harbor, the land surface was as much as 20 m below its present elevation immediately after ice retreat (~10 ka). Most of the rebound occurred during the first few thousand years, with slower uplift continuing today at roughly a millimeter per year.

Throughout this period, the land never exceeded its present height; it was continually rising toward today’s elevation. The interplay of this slow uplift with fluctuating lake levels determined where shorelines formed and left their mark in the strandplain.

---

## Strandplain Formation

A **strandplain** is a broad, gently sloping area built by successive beach ridges and swales formed along a receding or advancing shoreline. Each ridge marks a former lake or sea level, preserved as the water either advanced (transgressed) or retreated (regressed) across the land. Over thousands of years, these ridges accumulate, recording the lake’s oscillating levels in sand, gravel, and cobble.

At Au Train and Copper Harbor, strandplains record the sequence of the Holocene lake-level changes: rising and falling water left a series of parallel ridges trending roughly east–west, nearly parallel to the present Lake Superior shoreline. These ridges are sometimes called **beach ridges** or **strand ridges**, and together they form a complete record of the region’s coastal evolution.

---

## Swales and Shoreline Processes

Between the beach ridges lie **swales** — low, marshy or ponded depressions that represent the former backshore zones between successive beaches. Swales may collect organic material and water, forming peat or small wetlands. Over time, alternating deposition and erosion along the shifting shoreline create the rhythmic ridge-and-swale pattern typical of strandplains.

Two key geologic terms describe these shoreline shifts:

- **Transgression** — When the water level rises relative to the land, causing the shoreline to move **landward**.
- **Regression** — When the water level falls relative to the land, causing the shoreline to move **lakeward**.

Together, these processes of transgression and regression — combined with local sediment supply and wave energy — build the intricate topography of strandplains like those at Au Train.
